# Sepsis: A Life-Threatening Response to Infection

## What sepsis is
Sepsis is the body's extreme response to an infection. It is a life-threatening medical emergency.
Sepsis happens when an infection you already have triggers a chain reaction throughout the body.
Without fast treatment, sepsis can quickly lead to tissue damage, organ failure, and death. Almost
any infection can lead to sepsis, including infections of the lungs, urinary tract, skin, and gut.

## Warning signs and when to seek emergency care
Warning signs of sepsis include confusion or disorientation, extreme pain or discomfort, fever,
shivering, or feeling very cold, clammy or sweaty skin, a high heart rate or weak pulse, and
shortness of breath. A fever together with confusion, a very fast heart rate, fast breathing, or
clammy skin is especially concerning. Seek emergency care immediately if you have an infection that
is getting worse or you develop these warning signs — call 911 or go to the emergency room. Tell
the medical team if you think an infection is not improving, because early recognition and
treatment save lives.

## Who is at higher risk
Anyone can develop sepsis, but it is more common and more dangerous in some groups: adults 65 and
older, people with weakened immune systems, people with chronic conditions such as diabetes, lung
disease, or kidney disease, and people who have recently had a serious illness, surgery, or
hospital stay. Preventing infections — through vaccination, good wound care, and managing chronic
conditions — lowers the risk of sepsis. If you have an infection and start to feel much worse, do
not wait: trust your instincts, say "I am concerned about sepsis," and seek care immediately. Acting
fast on a worsening infection is what makes the difference.
