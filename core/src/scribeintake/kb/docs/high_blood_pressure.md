# High Blood Pressure and Hypertensive Crisis

## Understanding the numbers
A blood pressure reading has two numbers. The systolic number (the top or first number) is the
pressure when the heart beats and pumps blood. The diastolic number (the bottom or second number)
is the pressure when the heart rests between beats. For example, 120/80 means a systolic of 120 and
a diastolic of 80. Normal blood pressure is below 120/80. Elevated and high blood pressure are
defined by higher ranges, and blood pressure that stays high over time raises the risk of heart
attack, stroke, and kidney disease.

## Hypertensive crisis: when to seek emergency care
A blood pressure reading higher than 180 systolic or higher than 120 diastolic is dangerously high
and is called a hypertensive crisis. If your reading is this high, wait a few minutes and take it
again. If it is still that high, seek medical care right away. Call 911 if the very high reading
comes with chest pain, shortness of breath, a severe headache, vision changes, weakness, numbness,
trouble speaking, or confusion, because these can be signs that the high pressure is harming organs
such as the heart, brain, or kidneys. A very high reading without any of these symptoms still needs
same-day medical care.

## Measuring and managing blood pressure
Because blood pressure can vary, a single high reading is not always a crisis, and home readings
can sometimes be inaccurate if the cuff is the wrong size or you measured right after activity. If
a home monitor shows a very high number, sit quietly for five minutes and measure again before
deciding what to do. Taking blood pressure medicines as prescribed, lowering salt, staying active,
and not smoking all help keep blood pressure in a safe range over time. If you take blood pressure
medicine and have missed doses, tell your provider, because missed doses can cause readings to
climb. A home reading that is high should be confirmed in a clinic, but a hypertensive-crisis
reading with warning symptoms is always an emergency.
