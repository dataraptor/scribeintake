# Heart Attack: Warning Signs and When to Call 911

## Warning signs and symptoms
A heart attack happens when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is blocked. The most common
symptom is chest discomfort. It is often in the center or left side of the chest, and it usually
lasts more than a few minutes, or it goes away and comes back. The discomfort can feel like
uncomfortable pressure, squeezing, fullness, or pain. You may also feel discomfort in one or both
arms, the back, shoulders, neck, jaw, or the upper part of the stomach. Shortness of breath is
common and sometimes it is the only symptom; it may come before or along with the chest
discomfort. Other warning signs include breaking out in a cold sweat, nausea or vomiting, and
feeling lightheaded or unusually tired. Women are somewhat more likely than men to have shortness
of breath, nausea, and back or jaw pain.

## When to seek emergency care
Call 911 right away if you or someone else might be having a heart attack. Call even if you are
not sure that it is a heart attack. Minutes matter: without blood reaching the heart, the heart
muscle begins to die, but quick treatment can prevent or limit the damage. Do not drive yourself
to the hospital and do not wait to see if the symptoms go away. Emergency medical responders can
begin treatment on the way to the hospital. New chest pain or pressure that spreads to the arm,
jaw, or back, especially with sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath, should be treated as an
emergency.

## What to do while waiting for help
While you wait for emergency responders, stay as calm and still as you can, and sit or lie down in
a comfortable position. If you are not allergic to aspirin and your provider has not told you to
avoid it, chewing an aspirin while waiting can help, but do not delay calling 911 to find one. If
the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally, begin CPR if you are trained, or
follow the dispatcher's instructions for hands-only chest compressions until help arrives. Keep a
list of the person's medicines and allergies ready to give to the medical team, as this speeds
safe treatment.
