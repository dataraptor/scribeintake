# When to Use the Emergency Room, Urgent Care, or Your Provider

## Call 911 for life-threatening emergencies
If a person could die or be permanently disabled, it is an emergency — call 911. Call 911 for
choking, stopped breathing or severe shortness of breath, severe chest pain, a sudden inability to
speak, see, walk, or move, sudden weakness or drooping on one side of the body, a seizure, or
heavy bleeding that will not stop. For these problems, do not drive yourself; emergency responders
can start care on the way to the hospital.

## Go to the emergency department
Go to the emergency department for serious problems that are not immediately life-threatening but
still urgent. These include trouble breathing, fainting, a sudden or unusual severe headache, a
high fever with a stiff neck, deep wounds or possible broken bones, coughing or vomiting blood, a
severe allergic reaction, or a suspected poisoning or overdose.

## Use urgent care or call your provider
If your problem is not life-threatening and is not risking disability, but you are concerned and
cannot see your provider soon enough, go to an urgent care clinic. Urgent care is appropriate for
mild illnesses such as colds, flu, sore throats, and earaches, and for minor injuries such as
sprains, minor cuts and burns, and low-grade fevers. If you are not sure what to do, call your
provider or a nurse advice line for guidance based on your symptoms.
