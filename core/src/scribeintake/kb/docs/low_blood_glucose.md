# Low Blood Glucose (Hypoglycemia)

## What it is and the numbers
Low blood glucose, also called low blood sugar or hypoglycemia, happens when the level of glucose
in your blood drops below what is healthy for you. For many people with diabetes, a reading under
70 mg/dL means low blood glucose, though your target may be different. A reading below 54 mg/dL is
considered seriously low and needs immediate action. Low blood glucose is most common in people who
take insulin or certain diabetes medicines.

## Symptoms
Mild to moderate symptoms include feeling shaky or jittery, hungry, tired, dizzy or lightheaded,
sweaty, irritable, or confused. You may have a fast heartbeat or a headache. At night, low blood
glucose can cause nightmares, crying out, heavy sweating, or waking up tired and confused. If blood
glucose drops very low, you may lose consciousness or have a seizure.

## Treatment and when to call 911
For mild low blood glucose, eat or drink 15 to 20 grams of glucose or fast-acting carbohydrate
right away, such as glucose tablets, fruit juice, or regular (non-diet) soda, then check again in
15 minutes. Severe low blood glucose is an emergency. Glucagon, a hormone that raises blood glucose,
is the best treatment when a person cannot safely eat or drink, is unconscious, or is having a
seizure. Tell family and friends to call 911 right away after giving glucagon, or if no glucagon is
available.

## Causes and prevention
Low blood glucose is most often caused by too much insulin or diabetes medicine, skipping or
delaying meals, eating less than usual, drinking alcohol, or being more physically active than
usual. Checking your blood glucose regularly, eating meals on a consistent schedule, and carrying a
fast-acting source of sugar can help prevent lows. If you have frequent low blood glucose, talk
with your provider about adjusting your medicines. After any severe low, let your care team know so
they can help you avoid the next one. Knowing your own early warning signs and acting on them
quickly is the best way to keep a mild low from becoming a dangerous one.
