# Headache: When to Seek Emergency Care

## Most headaches are not dangerous
Headaches are very common. Most are tension-type or migraine headaches, and most people find
relief with rest, fluids, stress reduction, and over-the-counter pain relievers. A headache that
follows your usual pattern and gets better with these measures is usually not an emergency. Still,
certain features of a headache are warning signs that need prompt medical attention.

## Red-flag headaches that need emergency care
Let your health care provider know about sudden, severe headaches. Get medical help right away if
you have a headache that comes on suddenly and feels like the worst headache of your life, or a
headache that quickly becomes severe (a "thunderclap" headache). Seek emergency care for a
headache that comes with a stiff neck, fever, confusion, loss of consciousness, weakness, numbness,
trouble speaking, vision changes, or pain in the eye or ear. Get medical help right away if you
have a headache after a blow to the head. These features can point to a serious cause such as
bleeding in or around the brain or an infection of the brain's lining, and they should not wait.

## Common types of headache
Tension-type headaches are the most common. They often cause a dull, aching, pressing pain on both
sides of the head, sometimes described like a tight band, and they can come and go over days.
Migraines tend to cause throbbing pain, often on one side, and may come with nausea or sensitivity
to light and sound. These ordinary headaches usually respond to rest, fluids, stress reduction, and
over-the-counter pain relievers. Keeping track of when headaches happen and what seems to trigger
them can help you and your provider find the pattern. A new or different kind of headache, or one
that is much worse than usual, is worth mentioning to your provider even when it is not an
emergency.
